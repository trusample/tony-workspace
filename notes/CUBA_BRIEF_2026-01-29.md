## 3) My take: likely political trajectory (30 / 90 days)

### Base case (most likely)
**30 days:**
- Continued **rolling blackouts** and intermittent fuel scarcity remain the central driver of public frustration.
- The government likely prioritizes **stability + security posture**: selective detentions, preventing opposition visibility at diplomatic events, and strong messaging blaming the U.S./sanctions.
- Expect **no near-term “collapse”**; instead, persistent degradation (services, food/medicine availability) with periodic localized flare-ups.

**90 days:**
- Regime stability remains intact, but the state becomes **more brittle**: more frequent service failures, higher emigration pressure, and greater reliance on ad-hoc fuel procurement + any partner willing to transact.
- External pressure (U.S. maritime enforcement / sanctions) and Mexico’s posture become the main swing factors.

### Risk case (meaningfully worse)
**30–90 days:**
- A **multi-province grid shock** (major plant failure + low reserves) triggers longer blackouts, which in turn triggers **spontaneous protests**.
- State response: swift arrests, increased internet throttling, and tighter control of civil society.
- Potential secondary effects: **migration surge**, regional diplomatic incident(s), or a more confrontational U.S. posture.

### Upside case (better-than-expected)
**30 days:**
- Cuba secures **stopgap fuel** (cash purchases / intermediary deals) and manages outages down from “crisis peaks,” reducing protest risk.

**90 days:**
- Mexico/Russia/other flows stay sufficient to keep the grid limping; Havana implements limited **economic concessions** (import flexibility, partial price adjustments, local energy fixes) to reduce social pressure.

## 4) Triggers that would change the outlook (watchlist)

**Moves outlook toward risk:**
- Confirmed U.S. move toward a **naval blockade / expanded interdictions** affecting *non-Venezuelan* supply lines.
- Sustained UNE reports showing **>50%** peak-hour load shedding for multiple consecutive days *plus* visible multi-city protests.
- Verified reports of severe **telecom disruption** (ETECSA outages beyond normal load shedding) or broader internet shutdowns.

**Moves outlook toward upside:**
- Verified increased fuel inflows (Mexico or other suppliers) + measurable reduction in generation deficits.
- Concrete policy steps that improve household purchasing power or availability of essentials (not just announcements).

## 5) Bottom line (non-alarmist)
The core story remains **energy + fuel constraints** feeding economic stress. The U.S. is using Venezuela’s disruption to raise pressure and narrative momentum, but **rhetoric ≠ immediate regime change**. The near-term risk is not “collapse,” it’s **service failure → localized protests → repression**.
